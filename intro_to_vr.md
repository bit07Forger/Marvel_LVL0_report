## What is Virtual Reality?

Virtual Reality is a technology that places you inside a fully simulated 3D environment, 
making you feel like you are somewhere completely different. It works by stimulating 
human senses — sight, sound, and sometimes even touch and smell — to trick the brain 
into believing it is experiencing a different world.

---

## Types of Virtual Reality

**Non-Immersive VR** keeps you aware of your physical surroundings while interacting 
with a virtual environment. Video games are the most common example — you are in front 
of a screen, not inside the world.

**Semi-Immersive VR** pulls you partway in. Flight simulators used for pilot training 
are a good example — realistic enough to train on but not fully disconnected from 
reality.

**Fully Immersive VR** aims to replicate reality as closely as possible, simulating 
sight, sound, and sometimes smell. Car racing games that give you a physical sense of 
speed sit at this end of the spectrum. Truly complete immersion is still something the 
technology is working toward.

---

## VR vs AR

**VR** replaces reality entirely. You put on a headset and the real world disappears — 
what you see, hear, and sometimes feel is completely computer generated. Think of 
stepping into a Pokemon world where nothing around you is real.

**AR** layers digital information on top of the real world instead of replacing it. 
Pokemon Go is the obvious example — you are still walking around your neighbourhood, 
but your phone's camera overlays digital creatures onto the real environment. No headset 
required, just a camera and an app.

---

## Where the Technology is Headed

**Wireless headsets** are removing the cables and giving users freedom to move around 
without being tethered to a machine.

**Social VR** platforms like VRChat are letting people meet and interact inside shared 
virtual spaces, turning VR into something closer to a social network than a solo 
experience.

**Healthcare** is quietly becoming one of the more serious application areas — VR is 
being used in pain management, physical therapy, and mental health treatment with 
genuinely promising results.

**Haptic feedback and eye-tracking** are making experiences feel more natural. Eye 
tracking lets you navigate by simply looking, and haptic feedback adds a sense of touch 
to virtual objects.

**Project Phantom** is an interesting example of VR being used in scientific 
collaboration — researchers can explore 3D models of the Moon or Mars in a VR 
environment and work alongside mission teams in real time, bridging physical distance 
in a way traditional tools cannot.
